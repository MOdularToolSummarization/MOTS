[0] In 1816, the family moved to Boston, where Willis's father established the Boston Recorder and, nine years later, the Youth's Companion, the world's first newspaper for children. [1] In the latter part of the 1820s, Willis began contributing more frequently to magazines and periodicals. [2] Despite this popularity, he was censured by some critics for indiscretion in reporting private conversations. [3] On June 20, 1839, Willis's play Tortesa, the Usurer premiered in Philadelphia at the Walnut Street Theatre. [4] In 1842, Willis employed Harriet Jacobs, an escaped slave from North Carolina, as a house servant and nanny. [5] As a critic, Willis did not believe in including discussions of personalities of writers when reviewing their works. [6] While Willis was editor of the Evening Mirror, its issue for January 29, 1845, included the first printing of Poe's poem "The Raven" with his name attached. [7] Willis's wife Mary Stace died in childbirth on March 25, 1845. [8] In 1846, Willis settled near the banks of Canterbury Creek near the Hudson River in New York and named his new home Idlewild. [9] Willis arbitrarily refused to print the work of his sister Sara Willis ("Fanny Fern") after 1854, though she previously had contributed anonymous book reviews to the Home Journal. [10] Among his later works, following in his traditional sketches about his life and people he has met, were Hurry-Graphs (1851), Out-Doors at Idlewild (1854), and Ragbag (1855). [11] In July 1860, Willis took his last major trip.
