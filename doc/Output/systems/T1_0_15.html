[0] In 1747, approximately 100 men of suitable age to serve in the colonial militia lived in North Carolina west of present-day Hillsborough. [1] In 1755, Governor Arthur Dobbs ordered the construction of a fortified log structure for the protection of settlers in Rowan County from various Native American threats, including assaults from Cherokee, Catawba, Shawnee, and Delaware raiding parties. [2] By June 1756, Waddell had substantially completed construction on the fort. [3] In 1756, the North Carolina General Assembly petitioned King George II for assistance, stating that the frontier remained in a relatively defenseless state. [4] Between 1756 and 1760, Fort Dobbs was used as a base of operations for Waddell's company of militia. [5] In addition to warning nearby natives against attacking settlers in the Carolinas, Dobbs also charged Waddell with attempting to keep peace with the Catawba. [6] Decline and fall of Anglo-Cherokee relations [7] Several pro-French leaders among the Cherokee pushed for violent actions against British settlers, despite the opposition of pro-British Cherokee leaders. [8] At around the same time as this attack occurred, Cherokee war parties attacked Fort Loudoun, Fort Prince George, and Ninety-Six, South Carolina. [9] In 2006, a researcher affiliated with East Carolina University, Lawrence Babits, presented a study and a reconstruction plan that has been accepted by the Friends of Fort Dobbs, the 501(c)(3) nonprofit organization that supports the site, and the North Carolina Department of Cultural Resources. [10] The State of North Carolina maintains and operates the area as Fort Dobbs State Historic Site.
