[0] axonomy and naming [1] What is now recognized as a single morphologically variable taxon named Galerina marginata was once split into five distinct species. [2] Another species analysed in Gulden's 2001 study, Galerina pseudomycenopsis, also could not be distinguished from G. marginata based on ribosomal DNA sequences and restriction fragment length polymorphism analyses. [3] In the fourth edition (1986) of Singer's comprehensive classification of the Agaricales, G. marginata is the type species of Galerina section Naucoriopsis, a subdivision first defined by French mycologist Robert Kühner in 1935. [4] The specific epithet marginata is derived from the Latin word for "margin" or "edge", while autumnalis means "of the autumn". [5] The cap reaches 1.7 to 4 cm (0.67 to 1.6 in) in diameter. [6] The gills are typically narrow and crowded together, with a broadly adnate to nearly decurrent attachment to the stem and convex edges. [7] The spores measure 8–10 by 5–6 µm, and are slightly inequilateral in profile view, and egg-shaped in face view. [8] Another potential edible lookalike is the "velvet foot", Flammulina velutipes. [9] Habitat and distribution [10] Galerina marginata is a saprobic fungus, obtaining nutrients by breaking down organic matter. [11] Galerina marginata is widely distributed throughout the Northern Hemisphere, found in North America, Europe, Japan, Iran, continental Asia, and the Caucasus. [12] The toxins found in Galerina marginata are known as amatoxins. [13] Galerina marginata was shown in various studies to contain the amatoxins α-amanitin and γ-amanitin, first as G. venenata, then as G. marginata and G. autumnalis.
