[1] The Black Vulture (Coragyps atratus) also known as the American Black Vulture, is a bird in the New World vulture family whose range extends from the southeastern United States to Central Chile and Uruguay in South America. [2] Although a common and widespread species, it has a somewhat more restricted distribution than its compatriot, the Turkey Vulture, which breeds well into Canada and south to Tierra del Fuego. [3] Despite the similar name and appearance, this species is unrelated to the Eurasian Black Vulture. [4] The latter species is an Old World vulture in the family Accipitridae (which includes eagles, hawks, kites and harriers), whereas the American species is a New World vulture. [5] It is the only extant member of the genus Coragyps, which is in the family Cathartidae. [6] It inhabits relatively open areas which provide scattered forests or shrublands. [7] With a wingspan of 1.5 m (5 ft) the Black Vulture is a large bird though relatively small for a vulture. [8] It has black plumage, a featherless, grayish-black head and neck, and a short, hooked beak. [9] The Black Vulture is a scavenger and feeds on carrion, but will also eat eggs or kill newborn animals. [10] In areas populated by humans, it also feeds at garbage dumps. [11] It finds its meals either by using its keen eyesight or by following other (New World) vultures, which possess a keen sense of smell. [12] Lacking a syrinx—the vocal organ of birds—its only vocalizations are grunts or low hisses. [13] It lays its eggs in caves or hollow trees or on the bare ground, and generally raises two chicks each year, which it feeds by regurgitation. [14] In the United States, the vulture receives legal protection under the Migratory Bird Treaty Act of 1918. [15] This vulture also appeared in Mayan codices.
